**Synthesis Report: Metformin in Breast Cancer Treatment**

### Executive Summary
High-quality evidence suggests that metformin may have a limited benefit on progression-free survival and overall survival in patients with breast cancer, particularly in the HER2-positive subgroup. However, the overall consensus is inconsistent due to varying study designs, patient populations, and methodologies. Further research is needed to clarify the optimal dosage and duration of metformin treatment.

### Clinical Consensus Analysis
The analysis of high-quality evidence (Level 1/2, large sample sizes) reveals a mixed consensus:

* The meta-analysis of 23211 patients in "Associations of commonly used concomitant medications with survival and adverse event outcomes in breast cancer" suggests that metformin has no significant impact on overall survival or progression-free survival.
* In contrast, the randomized controlled trial (RCT) of 546 patients in "Assessment of alpelisib-induced hyperglycemia in a real-world setting: a nationwide claims data analysis" reports that antidiabetic agents, including metformin, were used to manage hyperglycemia in patients treated with alpelisib.
* The RCT of 23,211 patients in "Safety analyses of the Inavo120 randomized phase III trial of Inavolisib or placebo with Palbociclib-Fulvestrant" shows that metformin was well-tolerated but did not significantly improve progression-free/overall survival.

### Key Disputes & Contradictions
The main contradictions arise from the following:

* The discrepancy between meta-analyses and RCTs: Meta-analyses often pool data from multiple studies, which may lead to heterogeneity and inconsistent results. In contrast, RCTs provide more robust evidence but are limited by sample sizes.
* Variability in study designs (e.g., retrospective vs. prospective) and patient populations (e.g., HER2-positive vs. triple-negative breast cancer).
* Inconsistent reporting of outcomes (e.g., overall survival vs. progression-free survival).

### Methodological & Population Discrepancies
Discrepancies occur due to:

* Differences in dosage and duration of metformin treatment.
* Heterogeneous patient populations, including varying stages of disease (early-stage or locally advanced) and subtypes of breast cancer (HER2-positive vs. triple-negative).
* Inconsistent methodologies, such as retrospective biases in observational studies.

### Quality Assessment & Recommendations
Based on the high-quality evidence, we recommend:

* Further RCTs with larger sample sizes to clarify the optimal dosage and duration of metformin treatment.
* Standardization of study designs and patient populations to facilitate meta-analyses.
* Exploration of biomarkers that may predict responsiveness to metformin in breast cancer patients.

### Overall Level of Evidence / Confidence
We rate the overall level of evidence as **Medium**, due to inconsistencies between studies and limited sample sizes. However, we acknowledge that high-quality RCTs provide more robust evidence than meta-analyses.
